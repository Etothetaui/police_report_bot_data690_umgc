General legal and procedural guidance:

1. If a qualifying protection order is active and applies to the suspect, the suspect may be legally prohibited from possessing firearms.

2. If a person has a qualifying prior domestic violence conviction, that person may be legally prohibited from possessing firearms.

3. Firearms should only be documented, secured, or confiscated when legally permitted by agency policy and applicable procedure, such as when firearms are lawfully accessible at the scene, plainly visible, covered by consent, covered by a warrant, or otherwise lawfully recoverable.

4. If firearms are reported but are not lawfully recoverable at the scene, officers should document the reported firearm access, location, and any needed follow-up.

5. If the suspect is not present when officers arrive, the report should not state that an arrest was made at the scene. Officers should document attempts to locate the suspect and any needed follow-up.

6. Officers should approach scenes with caution, avoid unnecessary escalation, and remain alert for persons leaving the scene, weapons, injuries, witnesses, and immediate safety concerns.

7. If a protection order is known to exist, officers should document the order, the protected party, the restrained party, the relevant terms, how the suspect allegedly violated the order, and any available evidence of the violation.

8. Officers should not refuse to document a protection-order violation merely because the restrained party is no longer on scene or because the violation did not occur in the officer's presence.

9. For domestic violence or partner/family member assault incidents, officers should consider predominant-aggressor factors when relevant, including threats, intimidation, prior violence, injuries, witness statements, and the overall context of the incident.

10. Officers should avoid placing the victim in the position of deciding whether the suspect should be arrested. Arrest and enforcement decisions should be based on facts, probable cause, safety concerns, and applicable procedure.

11. Arrest decisions should not be based on marital status, property ownership, tenancy rights, promises that the conduct will stop, or the victim's emotional state.

12. Victim safety and protection should be treated as a priority. Reports should document safety concerns, victim needs, resources provided, and any immediate protective actions taken.

13. When applicable, officers should provide information about protection orders, shelter or advocacy services, local service providers, and safety planning.

14. When applicable, officers should use or document any required threat, danger, or risk assessment process.

15. Reports should document relevant facts such as the relationship between the parties, history of prior incidents, visible injuries, complaints of pain, emotional state, threats, witnesses, evidence, property damage, and scene observations.

16. If an arrest is not made when one might otherwise be expected, the report should clearly document the reason.

17. If stalking behavior is relevant to the selected crime type or checklist, officers should document repeated unwanted contact, surveillance, following, unwanted messages, unwanted gifts, threats, fear, safety concerns, and any stalking log or related evidence.

18. If strangulation is reported or suspected, officers should document the mechanism, duration if known, breathing impairment, voice changes, trouble swallowing, neck pain, loss of consciousness, nausea, headache, disorientation, visible injuries, non-visible symptoms, medical evaluation, and whether EMS or other medical care was requested or provided.

19. If sexual assault is reported or suspected, officers should document the disclosure, timeline, location, suspect information, victim services, medical or forensic response if applicable, evidence preservation, and any statements or observations relevant to the report.

20. Officers should document co-occurring conduct only when it is supported by the facts provided, observed evidence, or the selected checklist. Do not add unrelated crimes or legal conclusions merely because they commonly occur in similar incidents.

21. If the accused is in custody or otherwise subject to custodial interrogation, officers should follow required procedures before questioning.

22. Legal and procedural concepts should be written in plain language. Scenario facts and initial reports should avoid statute numbers, penal code citations, case-law citations, state-specific references, and court-specific legal conclusions unless the training exercise specifically requires them.
