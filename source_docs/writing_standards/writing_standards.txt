Here is the list of specific, actionable rules for police report writing:

1. Use active voice. The subject of the sentence must perform the action. Wrong: "Mr. Jones was arrested." Right: "I arrested Mr. Jones." Wrong: "The room was cleared by officers." Right: "I cleared the room with Officer Smith." Wrong: "Surveillance video was reviewed." Right: "I reviewed the surveillance video."
2. Never state conclusions without the observable facts that support them. Wrong: "The subject was drunk." Right: "Mr. Jones had bloodshot eyes, slurred speech, and was unsteady on his feet." Wrong: "The suspect seemed angry." Right: "The suspect raised his voice, slammed his fist on the table, and said 'I will kill you.'" Wrong: "The subject appeared to be having a psychotic episode." Right: "Ms. Jones told me she had not eaten or drunk anything for three days. Her clothing appeared dirty and soiled. She told me she was hearing voices telling her to harm herself."
3. Never write in future tense. Reports document what has happened, not what will happen. Wrong: "I will collect video footage on Monday." Right: "Ms. Jones agreed to send me the video footage." Wrong: "I plan to interview Mr. Jones next week." Right: "This case remains open for further investigation." Exception: documenting someone's past statement about a future action is acceptable. Right: "Ms. Brown told me she would send the video on Monday."
4. Quote victims, witnesses, and suspects exactly using quotation marks. Do not paraphrase direct quotes — editing someone's exact words may alter the meaning or diminish clarity.
5. Attribute every statement to its speaker. Wrong: "She said he had threatened her before." Right: "The victim stated that the suspect had threatened her on prior occasions."
6. Every factual claim must be accurate and complete. Unintentional errors or omissions can mislead prosecutors, judges, and juries. Do not assume the reader knows what you mean — state everything explicitly. Wrong: "After I detained Mr. Jones, he said he understood his rights." Right: "I detained Mr. Jones in handcuffs. Prior to questioning him, I read him his Miranda rights from my department-issued Miranda card."
7. Articulate legal justification for every police action. Every encounter must be categorized as consensual, investigative detention based on reasonable articulable suspicion, or arrest based on probable cause. The report must state the specific facts that justified the officer's actions. Wrong: "I arrived on scene and drew my gun." Right: "I arrived on scene and observed a subject matching the lookout description holding a machete. I immediately drew my pistol and commanded both subjects to the ground."
8. Address every element of the crime being investigated. Each crime has specific legal elements required for probable cause and prosecution. The narrative must satisfy every element. Wrong: A report describing a purse being grabbed without documenting force, threat, or intimidation does not satisfy the elements of robbery.
9. Never use unexplained police jargon, abbreviations, or agency-specific codes. Wrong: "29s returned negative." Right: "Dispatch told me the subject was not wanted." Wrong: "I made contact with the victim." Right: "I spoke with the victim." Wrong: "I assisted him to the ground." Right: "I grabbed the subject by the right arm with both hands and pulled him to the ground."
10. Write in chronological order. Describe events in the order they occurred, not in the order they were discovered or reported.
11. Answer the six basic questions for every report: Who, What, Where, When, Why, and How. Who: victim, witnesses, suspect, officer, who discovered the crime, who was interviewed, who marked evidence. What: offense type, suspect actions, officer actions, evidence, weapons. Where: offense location, evidence location, witness locations. When: time of incident, discovery, report, officer arrival, photos. How: how the offense was committed, how the arrest was made. Why: motive, why witnesses were reluctant, why suspect selected the target.
12. Use specific, concrete language rather than vague or abstract terms. Wrong: "The majority of witnesses said the suspect had blonde hair." Right: "Four of the five witnesses said the suspect had blonde hair." Wrong: "I told the victim I would call him in the near future." Right: "I told the victim I would call him on Thursday at approximately 1700 hours." Wrong: "A large crowd had gathered." Right: "Approximately 50 to 60 people were present when I arrived."
13. Use short sentences. Separate each idea into its own sentence. Do not combine several thoughts into one long sentence. Replace connective words like "and" and "but" with a period where possible. Wrong: "Mr. Jones told me that he had received a phone call from Elliott Jenkins, but he didn't remember the time of the call." Right: "Mr. Jones told me he had received a phone call from Elliott Jenkins. He did not remember the time of the call."
14. Use pronouns carefully. Every pronoun must refer clearly and unmistakably to its noun. Wrong: "When Jenkins became partners with Smith in May, he did not know he would be such a good cop." Right: "When Jenkins became Smith's partner in May, Jenkins did not know Smith would be such a good cop."
15. Use past tense throughout the narrative. Present tense is only acceptable when directly quoting a victim, witness, or suspect.
16. Do not avoid quoting crude or street language. The exact words of a victim, witness, or suspect must be documented verbatim — editing them may alter legal meaning.
17. All subjects mentioned in the narrative must be listed in the Subjects Section. All subjects listed in the Subjects Section must be mentioned in the narrative.
18. All vehicles mentioned in the narrative must be listed in the Vehicle Section. All vehicles listed in the Vehicle Section must be mentioned in the narrative.
19. Obtain and document secondary contact information for victims and witnesses in case they move before trial.
20. Document spontaneous statements verbatim as soon as they are made.
21. The narrative must establish four things in order: (1) the officer's standing to be at the location, (2) reasonable suspicion, (3) probable cause, (4) evidence sufficient for conviction beyond a reasonable doubt.
22. A detailed factual account of an officer's use of force or controversial action is the best protection against false accusations — not a liability. Do not omit descriptions of officer actions out of concern they may raise questions.
23. Avoid clichés. They add nothing to a report. Do not use expressions like "dead as a doornail," "hasty retreat," or "the whole nine yards."
24. Do not use unnecessarily long or technical words. Write to express, not to impress.
25. Proofread every report before submission. Check subject-verb agreement, verb tense consistency, spelling of all names, and all dates and times. Misspelled names cause misdirected subpoenas and missed court dates.

These rules are designed to help police officers write clear, concise, and accurate reports that effectively communicate the facts of an incident.
